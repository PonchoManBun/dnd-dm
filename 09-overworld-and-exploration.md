# 09 — Overworld & Exploration

## Node-Based Travel Map

The overworld is a **node-based map** — locations are connected points, not a continuous explorable space. The player selects a destination and travels along a path between nodes. The DM narrates the journey and may trigger encounters.

### Node Types

- **The Welcome Wench** — Always the central hub node.
- **Towns/Villages** — Shops, NPCs, side quests.
- **Dungeon Entrances** — Procedurally generated dungeons.
- **Wilderness Sites** — Camps, ruins, shrines, lairs.
- **Hidden Nodes** — Revealed by exploration, NPC hints, or map items.

## Travel

When the player selects a destination:

1. **DM narrates departure.** Weather, terrain, party condition.
2. **Random encounter check.** The DM rolls for encounters based on region danger and time of day.
3. **Encounter or arrival.** If an encounter triggers, it plays out on a combat/event map. Otherwise, the player arrives.

Travel time affects the day/night cycle. Longer routes are more dangerous but may lead to better rewards.

## Day/Night Cycle

Time passes as the player acts. Night brings:

- Reduced visibility (torch/darkvision rules apply on the overworld)
- More dangerous random encounters
- Different NPC availability in towns
- Nocturnal monsters

## Random Encounters

The DM generates encounters based on region, time, and narrative context. Not all encounters are combat — the player might find a wounded traveler, a merchant caravan, a mysterious shrine, or an ambush. The DM uses the encounter to advance faction storylines or introduce new threads.

## Map Item

Without a map item, the player sees no overworld view. The DM describes available directions verbally. Finding or buying a map unlocks the visual travel interface.
